## Purpose of This Document

This document defines the human outcomes Bridge is designed to achieve with precision sufficient for testing and decision-making. Every product feature, design choice, and release decision must be traceable to these outcomes.

---

## 1. Primary Human Outcome

```
Primary outcome: A person facing a communication barrier — whether caused by language, 
hearing, age, or system complexity — is able to have a real, meaningful exchange with 
someone they care about or a system they need to navigate, and feels genuinely understood.

Person affected: Anyone whose ability to communicate and connect is reduced by a barrier 
that technology can responsibly remove.

Observable signal that the outcome is occurring:
- The person initiates conversations or appointments they previously avoided due to the barrier
- After using Bridge, both participants in a conversation describe feeling like they 
  understood each other — not just the literal words
- The person navigates an appointment or system with confidence they did not previously have
- The technology is not mentioned when someone describes the conversation or experience

Observable signal that the outcome is NOT occurring:
- People use Bridge but describe the experience as "just using a tool"
- Conversations feel transactional rather than relational
- Users report that the process of using Bridge interrupts the emotional quality of the exchange
- People stop using Bridge because it made them feel more aware of the barrier, not less
```

## 2. Companion-Specific Human Outcomes

### 2.1 Bridge Translation

```
Outcome: A person separated from someone they love by a language barrier is able to have 
a real, meaningful conversation with them — where both parties feel genuinely understood.

Who benefits: Anyone maintaining a close personal relationship across a language or cultural 
gap — immigrants, diaspora communities, multilingual households, internationally distributed families.

Observable: Both participants describe feeling understood, not just correctly translated.
```

### 2.2 Bridge Appointment

```
Outcome: A person who faces a language or system barrier when navigating appointments — 
medical, legal, administrative — is able to participate fully, understand what is happening, 
and communicate what they need.

Who benefits: People who face language or complexity barriers when interacting with 
healthcare providers, legal systems, government offices, or other formal institutions.

Observable: The person is able to complete the appointment with clarity and without 
requiring a bilingual family member or advocate as intermediary.
```

### 2.3 Bridge Hearing

```
Outcome: A person who is Deaf or hard of hearing is able to participate fully in conversations 
and environments that would otherwise exclude or disadvantage them.

Who benefits: People with hearing loss or deafness — both those who are Deaf by culture 
and identity, and those with acquired hearing loss.

Observable: The person can have real-time communication in contexts that previously required 
a third-party interpreter or were inaccessible.
```

### 2.4 Bridge Independence

```
Outcome: An older adult who faces communication or system complexity barriers is able to 
navigate daily life — appointments, communications, information — with confidence and without 
dependence on a family member to manage these tasks for them.

Who benefits: Older adults whose independence in daily communication is reduced by 
technological complexity, language shifts in their community, or sensory change.

Observable: The older adult reports handling a daily task independently that they previously 
needed help with. Family members report reduced anxiety about the older adult's communication needs.
```

### 2.5 Bridge Legacy

```
Outcome: Families are able to record, preserve, and share stories and memories across 
language and generational barriers — so that what an older generation knows is accessible 
to younger generations who may not share their language or have many years left together.

Who benefits: Multigenerational families where stories exist in one language and the 
people who most want to hear them speak another; families with aging elders who hold 
irreplaceable history.

Observable: A family has preserved a story or memory that would not have survived the 
language gap, and younger generations describe feeling closer to family history as a result.
```

## 3. What Bridge Must Not Do to People

```
- Must not make any person feel that their communication barrier is their failure
- Must not surface errors, difficulties, or limitations in ways that embarrass or undermine confidence
- Must not store or expose conversation content in ways the user has not explicitly authorized
- Must not incentivize longer or more frequent usage through notifications, streaks, or 
  comparison features
- Must not make any participant in a conversation feel surveilled or recorded without 
  clear, informed consent
- Must not present AI-generated language as the person's own words without their awareness
- Must not create dependence where independence is the goal
```

---

## 4. Outcome vs. Engagement Distinction

```
What counts as success: A person able to communicate, connect, or navigate something 
they could not before. A relationship maintained or strengthened. A story preserved. 
An appointment attended with dignity.

What does NOT count as success: Time in app. Message volume. Daily active users. 
Retention rate. These metrics can grow while the human outcome deteriorates — someone 
opening an app frequently is not evidence they are connecting more meaningfully.

The only valid measure of Bridge's success: Do the people Bridge serves feel 
more connected, more capable, and more understood than they did before Bridge?
```
